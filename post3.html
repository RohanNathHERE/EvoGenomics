The Harmful Effects of Indian Cinema: A Critical Perspective

Published on September 13, 2024

Indian cinema has long been a defining element of the country's cultural landscape, but its impact on society is not without its dark sides. From perpetuating patriarchal norms and glorifying eve-teasing to fostering a culture of blind celebrity worship, Indian cinema has played a significant role in shaping social attitudes and behaviors in harmful ways. This article delves into the history of Indian cinema, citing examples from recent movies, to explore its negative effects on Indian society and crime.

A Brief History of Indian Cinema

Indian cinema, dating back to the early 1900s, began with Dadasaheb Phalke's silent film "Raja Harishchandra" in 1913. Since then, the film industry has grown exponentially, producing thousands of films annually in various languages. While Indian cinema has significantly contributed to entertainment, art, and culture, it has also perpetuated certain regressive ideologies and stereotypes, which continue to impact society today.

Perpetuation of Patriarchy

Indian films often glorify patriarchal values, presenting male dominance as the norm. For instance, in many Bollywood blockbusters, female characters are portrayed as dependent, submissive, or even voiceless, while male protagonists are shown as their saviors. A prime example is the 1995 film "Dilwale Dulhania Le Jayenge," which, despite its iconic status, promotes the idea that a woman's choices are secondary to her father's or lover's decisions.

More recent films, like "Kabir Singh" (2019), have taken this a step further. The film's male protagonist exhibits aggressive and violent behavior towards women, and his actions are portrayed as passionate love rather than abusive behavior. Such narratives reinforce toxic masculinity and normalize violence against women, contributing to a culture where gender-based violence is trivialized.

Glorification of Eve-Teasing

Indian cinema has a long history of romanticizing eve-teasing, portraying it as a legitimate way for men to express love or affection. Films like "Raanjhanaa" (2013) and "Badrinath Ki Dulhania" (2017) show male characters persistently pursuing female characters, ignoring their consent or discomfort. Instead of condemning such behavior, these films often present it as harmless fun or endearing persistence, effectively endorsing a culture of harassment.

This portrayal has real-world implications. Studies have shown that such depictions in films contribute to the normalization of eve-teasing, making it seem socially acceptable or even desirable. As a result, young men may be more likely to engage in such behavior, believing it to be a socially endorsed way of courtship, while women are forced to navigate an environment where their autonomy is regularly undermined.

Blind Celebrity Worship

The Indian film industry has also cultivated a culture of blind celebrity worship. Fans idolize film stars to an extreme degree, often mimicking their behavior and lifestyles. This phenomenon can have detrimental effects, as celebrities' actions and choices become aspirational models, regardless of their moral or ethical implications.

The case of actor Sanjay Dutt, who was convicted of illegal possession of arms but continues to enjoy immense popularity and support, illustrates this trend. Similarly, Salman Khan, despite his criminal charges, remains one of the most adored celebrities in India. The blind reverence for such personalities sends a problematic message: that fame and power can absolve one of accountability, perpetuating a culture where the law is selectively applied.

Impact on Society and Crime

The negative influence of Indian cinema is not confined to social attitudes alone. There is evidence to suggest that its impact extends to criminal behavior as well. For example, the glorification of vigilante justice in films like "Singham" (2011) and "Dabangg" (2010) reinforces the idea that taking the law into one's hands is justified. This has been linked to a rise in mob violence and extrajudicial actions across the country.

Additionally, the depiction of women as objects of desire and male entitlement in numerous films has been linked to increased cases of sexual harassment and assault. According to a report by the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB), there has been a consistent rise in crimes against women, which some sociologists argue is partly fueled by media representations that degrade women and normalize aggression towards them.

Conclusion

While Indian cinema has certainly evolved over the years, its harmful impact on social norms and behaviors cannot be ignored. By perpetuating patriarchal values, glorifying eve-teasing, and fostering a culture of blind celebrity worship, it has contributed to the perpetuation of regressive attitudes and even criminal behavior. It is imperative for filmmakers, audiences, and society at large to critically examine the content being produced and consumed, and to demand a more responsible portrayal of characters, relationships, and social dynamics.

Until then, Indian cinema will continue to be both a reflection of society's darker aspects and a contributor to their persistence.
